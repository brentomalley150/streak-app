AI Architecture

How agentic AI is used

Owner: Kate O'Malley · The split: rules for the kid's game, agentic AI for the parent's analysis · Updated: May 30, 2026

1 The principle

Use determinism where fairness, speed, and safety matter; use agentic AI where judgment and synthesis matter. The split is the architecture.

Kid game · rules-based

The competition must be deterministic

Points, streaks, ranks, and leaderboards have to be exact and un-gameable, run instantly on-device/offline, never expose a child to model free-text, keep a minor's data local, and cost nothing per tap.

Parent analysis · agentic

Turning activity into insight is judgment

Synthesizing raw logs into "what happened and what to do" — diagnosing against the hypothesis and recommending the next action — is exactly the judgment work an agent is for.

2 Why the kid side stays rules-based

  • Fairness. A competition's scoring must be exact and reproducible — an LLM would be inconsistent and trivially gameable by kids.
  • Speed & offline. The dashboard runs on-device (localStorage); rules compute instantly with no network.
  • Safety. No live model free-text reaches a child — no hallucination, no inappropriate content, no prompt-injection surface.
  • Privacy. A minor's activity stays local by default (COPPA-friendly), nothing sent to a model to play the game.
  • Cost. Every tap calling an LLM would be expensive; rules are free at any scale.

3 What makes the parent side agentic

A weekly summary written by an LLM is an AI feature. It becomes agentic when the system runs a multi-step loop with judgment and proposed actions — not just text generation.

The parent-analysis loop:

  1. Retrieve — this week's activity log, the spring baseline (MAP RIT 185, Lexile, writing %), and research-backed targets.
  2. Diagnose — interpret signals against the hypothesis (e.g., "minutes on pace, writing skipped — and writing is the lever for comprehension").
  3. Prioritize — surface the one thing that matters this week, not ten metrics.
  4. Propose — a concrete next action plus a draft nudge.
  5. Confirm-before-action — the parent approves; only then does it write back to the kid app (set the week's focus, add a bonus challenge, schedule a reminder).

5 Guardrails

Must hold across every response
  • Ground every claim in the logged numbers — no hallucinated progress.
  • The fall-score projection is an estimate, never stated as a promise.
  • No clinical or diagnostic inferences (e.g., "dyslexia") — escalate to "worth raising with his teacher."
  • Supportive tone; never shame the child or the parent.
  • A minor's data is consent-gated and never shared across families.
  • Confirm-before-action on anything that writes back to the kid app.

6 Where AI touches the kid side

AI can still improve the kid experience — but only batch, server-side, curated, and content-filtered, never live.

  • Book recommendations at the right level and interest — pre-computed and filtered, served into the rules engine.
  • Comprehension questions generated and reviewed ahead of time, then cached.
  • Adaptive difficulty tuned offline from aggregate data.

The child never talks to a live model; the rules engine only ever serves pre-approved, safe options.

7 The riskiest assumption & how we'll know it works

The bet isn't "can we summarize data" — it's whether the agent's analysis is accurate and trustworthy enough that parents act on it, and whether that actually moves the fall score. The MVP is one cadence (the weekly digest) for one cohort, with an eval harness.
