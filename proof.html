What We've Already Proven

A reference for the year-round Streak vision: what the SummerStreak experiment has demonstrated so far, what's still open, and how this informs every Phase 2/3/4 decision.

As of June 2026 · Updated as new data lands

The hypothesis we set out to test

If a struggling 4th-grader completes 15 min reading + 50 words writing + 5 min math daily for 12 summer weeks, supported by gamification + social competition + parent visibility, fall MAP Reading RIT will improve by 8+ points over the spring baseline (185 → 193+) — and the kid will actually open the app voluntarily.

Two embedded claims:

  1. Behavior claim: A kid who wouldn't read on their own will engage with a gamified dashboard.
  2. Outcome claim: That engagement produces measurable reading-score improvement on a standardized assessment.

The behavior claim can be falsified in weeks. The outcome claim takes until September.

Identity, sync, and anti-gaming

  • Google Sign-In — stable UID per parent across all devices
  • Multi-profile per Google account — siblings on one device, fully separated
  • Firebase-backed canonical weekly winners — every dashboard agrees on who won
  • Per-week point adjustments bucket — lifetime vs. weekly views stay consistent
  • One-click-per-day on every points-earning action — no spam-clicking
  • Defensive clamp: weekly ≤ lifetime, always
  • Days Done auto-filter: excludes empty entries created by calendar navigation

That's a complete, production-quality kid app delivered for $0 in three days of focused build + four weeks of iteration. Pre-AI, this was a 6-week sprint with a team of three.

What we observed — qualitative

Things that aren't in the data but are worth recording:

  • Theme picker mattered more than expected. Sebastian picked Chess, the gaming-leaning kids picked Gaming, and they each got prompts tailored to their interest. Removed the "ugh, this isn't for me" friction that one-size-fits-all kid apps trigger.
  • Parents iterated alongside the kid. Real bug reports came in within hours of features shipping. The build-with-the-user-in-the-loop pattern compressed normal QA cycles to minutes.
  • The Past Champions card created a moment. Once it was live, kids cared about being on it. A feature that wouldn't have shown up in a spec written in advance — emerged from the kid's reaction to seeing their name in print.
  • One-click-per-day locks weren't paternalistic, they were respectful. When the original "tap to toggle" approach let kids spam points, the kids themselves felt weird about it. Adding the lock made the points feel real.

What we have NOT proved yet

The outcome claim is still open. The September MAP RIT retest is the keystone. Until that number lands, we have strong engagement, strong adoption, strong weekly cycle, and a 4-week consistent reading habit — but the hypothesis that this translates to score improvement is unverified. If September shows +8 RIT or better, the full hypothesis is proven and every Phase 2/3/4 decision rests on solid ground. If it doesn't move, the engagement was real but the outcome claim needs revision. Either result is content; the unhonest version is the loss.

What this proves for the Streak vision

The SummerStreak experiment proves three things that all transfer to the year-round Streak vision:

✅The engine works
Daily activity card + points + ranks + leaderboards + prizes + weekly challenges + parent controls = a loop that 10-year-olds will voluntarily engage with. The exact same engine, with different content slots, works for any kid habit — math, music, mindfulness, homework. Phase 2 doesn't require re-proving the loop; it requires writing new content into a proven shell.
✅The build economics work
If one parent + AI can ship a complete, multi-family, real-time-synced kid app for $0 in 3 days, then iterating to 5+ tracks is a manageable scope — not a "we need to raise $1M" project. Year-round expansion is achievable as a solo build with the existing tooling stack.
✅The acquisition story works
"Parent vibe-coded this for their kid" is the strongest organic acquisition narrative in our hand. Drives sharing among PM-curious parents who amplify, credibility with parents of struggling readers because it wasn't built by a startup chasing metrics, and press/community interest because the meta-story is genuinely novel. Each is independent of which track is active — they scale across the platform.

Honest limitations the vision must account for

Sample size: 6 enrolled kids is not a study. Engagement signals are real but generalizability is unproven.
Survivor bias on the parent side: every parent who enrolled has a personal connection. Going wider requires testing acquisition from a cold audience.
No COPPA-grade infrastructure yet: works for friends-and-family pilot, requires real consent flow for public launch.
Outcome lag: any reading-improvement claim takes 8-12 weeks minimum to validate. The product can't promise results faster than the underlying biology of habit formation.
Single-dev velocity: scales until it doesn't. Phase 3+ likely requires another contributor or a more opinionated framework.

Bottom line

The summer experiment has already proven the behavior loop, the economics, and the story. What it hasn't proven yet is the outcome.

The vision pivot to Streak doesn't depend on a September win to be the right move — it depends on the behavior loop being real, which it already is. But the September data point determines whether the brand stands on "we built something kids actually use" (always true) or "we built something kids use AND it moves real outcomes" (still being tested).

Plan for both. Build for the second.
